Bispectrum & 3PCF




  • Lowest order statistic sensitive to LSS --> It probes the phases too!
  • The 3PCF, $\zeta$, for a set of points (or galaxies) in real space is defined as the excess probability, over random, of finding three galaxies at the vertices of a triangle with sides $r_{12}, r_{23}, and r_{31}$, compared to a random distribution.
  • 3PCF: $\zeta(r_{12}, r_{23}, r_{31}) = \langle \delta(\mathbf{x})\delta(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{r}_{12})\delta(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{r}_{31}) \rangle$
  • Bispectrum: $B(\mathbf{k}_1, \mathbf{k}_2, \mathbf{k}_3) = \langle \delta(\mathbf{k}_1) \delta(\mathbf{k}_2) \delta(\mathbf{k}_3) \rangle$; $\mathbf{k}_1 + \mathbf{k}_2 + \mathbf{k}_3 = 0$
Source: Sefusatti & Scoccimarro 2005

2 distributions that look different has same 2 point statistics, but different 3 point statistics!

Higher order than Bispectrum: 3x2 point and trispectrum

PDF



    While the $\mathrm{n}$-point correlation function describe the relationship between n-points within the dataset, the PDF gives the probability of finding a value within a specific range.

    PDf is obtained using histogram.

    $PDF(x_i) = \frac{n_i}{N \Delta x_i}$

    Use different smoothing scales to extract more information!

$\ell_1$-norm



    Gives the sum of the absolute values of the pixels in each bin.

    $L_1^{(bin)} = \sum_{i=1}^{N_{bin}}|x_i|$

    Use different smoothing scales to extract more information!

Peak and Void statistics



    Identify the regions where we have high density and low density

    Peak statistics: Peak count, Peak Height Distribution, Peak clustering, Peak Background

    Void Statistics: Void size distribution, Void abundance, Void clustering, Void profiles etc..

    Use different smoothing scales to extract more information!

Is that it?



    No! We are biased.

    Galaxy bias: The difference in spatial distribution between visible galaxies and the underlying dark matter due to the complex processes governing galaxy formation.

    Redshift space distortions: Apparent anisotropies in the spatial distribution of galaxies caused by their peculiar velocities along the line of sight, affecting redshift measurements.

    Noise: Random fluctuations in observational data that can obscure or mimic true cosmological signals.

    Systematics: Non-random errors introduced by observational instruments, data processing, or analysis techniques that can systematically skew results.

    Modeling bias: Discrepancies between theoretical models and the actual Universe that arise from oversimplified assumptions or incomplete physics.

    Baryonic effects: Influences on the cosmic structure from normal (non-dark) matter, including stars, gas, and galaxies, which affect the distribution and evolution of matter.
