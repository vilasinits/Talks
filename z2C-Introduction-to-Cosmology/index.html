Structure of the Universe


The FLRW Metric Line Element

The Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric describes a homogeneous and isotropic universe. The line element for this metric, assuming a flat spatial geometry, is given by: $$ds^2 = -dt^2 + a^2(dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2)$$

  • $ds^2$ is the spacetime interval,
  • $dt^2, dx^2, dy^2, dz^2$ are the differentials of time and spatial coordinates,
  • $a$ is the scale factor, which describes how distances in the universe expand over time.

Dynamics of the Universe

The dynamics of the universe is determined by the Einstein equation:

$$G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G \, T_{\mu\nu}$$

This relates the Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu}$ (a measure of the "spacetime curvature" of the FRW universe) to the stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$ (a measure of the "matter content" of the universe).

Friedmann Equations

The Friedmann equations are derived from the Einstein equation for the FLRW metric. They describe the evolution of the scale factor $a(t)$ with time $t$:

$$\left( \frac{\dot{a}}{a} \right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3} \rho - \frac{k c^2}{a^2}$$ $$\frac{\ddot{a}}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3} \left(\rho + \dfrac{3P}{c^2}\right)$$
  • The first Friedmann equation relates the expansion rate of the universe to the energy density $\rho$ and the spatial curvature $k$.
  • The second Friedmann equation relates the acceleration of the expansion to the energy density $\rho$ and the pressure $P$.

Introducing Small Perturbations

In cosmological perturbation theory, we introduce small deviations to study the formation of structures in the universe. For instance, the density $\rho(t, \boldsymbol{x})$ can be expressed as:

$$\rho(t, \boldsymbol{x}) = \bar{\rho}(t) + \delta\rho(t, \boldsymbol{x})$$

Similarly, for pressure and velocity, we have:

$$P(t, \boldsymbol{x}) = \bar{P}(t) + \delta P(t, \boldsymbol{x})$$ $$\boldsymbol{v}(t, \boldsymbol{x}) = \bar{\boldsymbol{v}}(t) + \delta\boldsymbol{v}(t, \boldsymbol{x})$$
  • $\bar{\rho}(t), \bar{P}(t), \bar{\boldsymbol{x}}(t)$ represent the average (background) density, pressure, and velocity,
  • $\delta\rho(t, \boldsymbol{x}), \delta P(t, \boldsymbol{x}), \delta\boldsymbol{v}(t, \boldsymbol{x})$ are the perturbations in density, pressure, and velocity at time $t$ and position $\boldsymbol{x}$.

General Form of Perturbed Metric

The metric with small perturbations can be generally expressed as:

$$ds^2 = -(1+2A)dt^2 + 2aB_i dx^i dt + a^2(\delta_{ij} + h_{ij})dx^i dx^j$$
  • $A$ and $B_i$ are scalar and vector perturbations affecting the time components,
  • $h_{ij}$ represents tensor perturbations to the spatial components,
  • $a$ is the scale factor, and $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta indicating the unperturbed spatial metric components.

Simplified Perturbed Metric for Scalar Perturbations

For scalar perturbations, the line element simplifies to:

$$ds^2 = -dt^2(1+2\Phi) + a^2(1+2\Psi)(dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2)$$
  • $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are the gravitational potentials related to scalar perturbations,
  • This form is particularly useful for analyzing the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) and large-scale structure.

Perturbed Stress-Energy Tensor

We write the perturbed stress-energy tensor as:

  • For the component $T_{00}$: $T_{00} = \bar{\rho}(\xi) + \delta\rho$
  • For the component $T_{i0}$: $T_{i0} = \left[ \bar{\rho}(\xi) + \bar{P}(\xi) \right] v_i$
  • For the component $T_{ij}$: $T_{ij} = \left[ \bar{P}(\xi) + \delta P \right] \delta_{ij} + \Pi_{ij}$

where:

  • $\bar{\rho}(\xi)$ and $\bar{P}(\xi)$ represent the average density and pressure in the background spacetime,
  • $\delta\rho$ and $\delta P$ are the perturbations in density and pressure, respectively,
  • $v_i$ represents the velocity field of the perturbations,
  • $\Pi_{ij}$ represents the anisotropic stress perturbations,
  • $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta, indicating that the pressure perturbation is isotropic in the absence of $\Pi_{ij}$.

Evolution of perturbation modes



    The evolution of these perturbation modes are given by the following equations:

    • Poisson Equation: $\nabla^2 \Phi = 4 \pi G a^2 \bar{\rho} \delta$
    • Equation of state: $\delta P = c_s^2 \delta \rho$
    • Continuity equation: $\frac{\partial \delta}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (1 + \delta) \mathbf{v} = 0$
    • Euler equation: $\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{v} = -\frac{\nabla \Phi}{a} - \frac{\nabla \delta P}{\rho a}$

    What leads to the growth of perturbations?

    • Gravitatinal Instability: Regions of slightly higher density exert a stronger gravitational pull compared to their surroundings. As a result, these regions attract more matter from their surroundings, leading to an increase in their density. This process is known as gravitational instability.

    • Jeans Instability: The concept of gravitational instability is closely related to the Jeans instability criterion in astrophysics, which describes how perturbations below a certain scale (the Jeans length) can grow due to gravity overcoming pressure forces that otherwise would counteract contraction.

Evolution of perturbation modes - Role of universe's content



  • Radiation dominated: High pressure of radiation collapses gravitational collapse ---> Slow growth of $\delta$
  • Matter dominated: Low pressure support ---> Effective & amplified growth
  • DE dominated: Accelerated expansion ---> Slowed growth of $\delta$

$\Lambda$CDM

Standard Model: $\Lambda$CDM


$\Lambda$CDM is a cosmological model of the universe with three main components:
  • $\Lambda$ : The cosmological constant, representing the energy density of the vacuum (dark energy),
  • CDM : Cold Dark Matter, a form of matter that interacts gravitationally but not electromagnetically,
  • Baryons : Ordinary matter composed of protons, neutrons, and electrons.

The $\Lambda$CDM model is consistent with a wide range of cosmological observations, including the CMB, large-scale structure, the accelerated expansion of the universe, and the observed abundances of light elements.

$\Lambda$CDM - Contents of the Universe



$\Lambda$CDM - Guiding Principles

  • General Relativity is the correct theory of gravity & $\Lambda$ accounts for the accelerated expansion of the universe

  • Flat spatial geometry of the universe ($k = 0$)

  • Gaussian initial conditions → density fluctuations in the early universe follow a Gaussian distribution

  • Cosmological principle: universe is homogeneous and isotropic on large scales, described by the FLRW metric

  • The evolution of the universe is governed by the Friedmann equations

$\Lambda$CDM - Independent Parameters

$\Lambda$CDM requires only 6 independent parameters to completely specify the cosmological model. The specific set is somewhat open to choice. There are many more parameters. All of these are inferred from different observations.

*Reionisation optical depth is cumulative likelihood that photons from the cosmic microwave background will interact with free electrons during the epoch of reionization.

From Implications to inference

    Hubble constant: can be determined through observations of distant galaxies and supernovae.

    Total Matter Density ($\Omega_m$): can be inferred from the clustering of galaxies, cosmic shear measurements (weak gravitational lensing), and the cosmic microwave background (CMB) anisotropies.

    Dark Energy Density ($\Omega_\lambda$): can be measured is measured through supernovae, baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO), and the CMB.

    Baryonic Matter Density $\Omega_b$: can be determined from Big Bang nucleosynthesis constraints and CMB anisotropies, which are sensitive to the amount of ordinary matter in the early universe.

    Amplitude of Initial Fluctuations ($A_s$) and Scalar Spectral Index ($n_s$): are inferred from the CMB anisotropies and the large-scale structure of the universe. The power spectrum of the CMB provides a direct measurement of these initial conditions.
