    Convergence \( \kappa \)

    The convergence field \( \kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \) quantifies the isotropic focusing of light bundles by foreground matter; it is proportional to the projected mass density.

    As a Line-of-sight (LOS) projection of the 3D overdensity:

    \[ \kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \int_{0}^{\chi_s}\! d\chi\; W(\chi)\,\delta(\chi\,\boldsymbol{\theta},\chi),\qquad W(\chi)=\frac{3H_0^2\Omega_m}{2c^2}\,\frac{\chi(\chi_s-\chi)}{a(\chi)\,\chi_s}. \]

    • Encodes both geometry (via \( \chi, a(\chi) \)) and growth (via \( \delta \)).
    Simulated \( \kappa(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \) map.

    …but is this sufficient?


    • Efficient, but lose non-Gaussian info.
    • Non-Gaussian structure lives in higher orders.
    • To access it → go beyond 2-pt: Higher-Order Statistics (HOS) (bispectrum, peak counts, Minkowski Functionals etc).

    Beyond the 2-point: higher-order statistics (HOS)

    Why “higher”?
    • 2-pt measures the second moment (variance); sufficient only if the information is Gaussian.
    • LSS is non-Gaussian → information lies beyond variance.

    ⟹ Polyspectra (bispectrum, trispectrum) quantify Fourier-mode coupling; morphology-based HOS (peaks, Minkowski) capture real-space non-Gaussian structure.

    Wavelet $\ell_1$-norm


    Definition
    \[ \boxed{\; w_{\theta_j}(x,y) = \big\langle \kappa,\ \Upsilon_{\theta_j,x,y} \big\rangle = \sum_{m,n}\ \kappa(m,n)\, \Upsilon\!\left(\frac{m-x}{\theta_j},\,\frac{n-y}{\theta_j}\right) \;} \] \[ \boxed{\; w_{\theta_j} = \big\langle \kappa,\ \xi_{\theta_{j+1}} \big\rangle - \big\langle \kappa,\ \xi_{\theta_j} \big\rangle \;} \] \[ \text{Dyadic scales: }\ \theta_j = 2^{\,j-1}\,\theta_1,\qquad W_\kappa=\{\,w_{\theta_1},\ldots,w_{\theta_J}\,\}. \]
    \[ \mathrm{S}_{j,i}\;=\;\big\{\, w_{j,k}/B_i \;<\; w_{j,k} \;<\; B_{i+1} \,\big\} \]
    \[ \boxed{\; \ell^{\,j,i}_{1} \;=\; \sum_{u=1}^{\#\mathrm{coef}(\mathrm{S}_{j,i})} \big|\,\mathrm{S}_{j,i}[u]\,\big| \;=\; \left\| \mathrm{S}_{j,i} \right\|_{1} \;} \]
    Here \(w_{j,k}\) are wavelet coefficients at scale \(\theta_j\); bins \([B_i,B_{i+1})\) select coefficient ranges.
    Why this summary?
    • information encoded in all pixels
    • automatically includes peaks and voids
    • multi-scale approach
    • avoids the problem of defining peaks and voids
    • Starlet wavelet -> B3 spline as scaling function
    • The starlet $\ell_1$-norm outperform peak and void statistics (Ajani et al. 2021).

    Why Large-Deviation Theory?

    Large-Deviation Theory (LDT): a framework to describe the probability of rare events by quantifying the exponential decay of probabilities in the tails of distributions.

    Gaussian approximations fail in the tails - where rare events dominate

    Part 1- Takeaways

    In a nutshell
    • Filter choice matters: compact, compensated (starlet) ⇒ less leakage, cleaner HOS.
    • LDT → PDF → \( \ell_1 \): fast, simulation-light predictions that match sims in the mildly non-linear regime.
    • Wavelet \( \ell_1 \): scale-dependent, localized summary capturing non-Gaussian information.
    • Practical swaps: B3/starlet window works when power is central-ℓ dominated.
    Systematics remain the challenge: current theory does not fully include shear/calibration/masking/IA, etc. Forward simulations are still the viable path to model and mitigate these effects.

    Theory validation pipeline for wavelet \( \ell_1 \)-norm

    Likelihood used

    We keep the covariance fixed at the fiducial cosmology and evaluate the mean with a GPR at each sampled \( \theta \).

    \[ \ln \mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; -\tfrac12\Big[ (\mathbf d_{\rm obs}-\mu_\theta)^{\!\top}\, \mathbf C_{\rm fid}^{-1}\, (\mathbf d_{\rm obs}-\mu_\theta) \;+\; \ln\!\det \mathbf C_{\rm fid} \;+\; n\ln(2\pi) \Big] . \]
    • \(\mathbf d_{\rm obs}\): measured wavelet \(\ell_1\) DV, \(\;\mu_\theta\): GPR prediction at sampled \(\theta\).
    • \(\mathbf C_{\rm fid}\): covariance estimated at fiducial cosmology (kept fixed for inference).

    Theory validation on cosmo-SLICS (wavelet \( \ell_1 \))

    Validation metric & gate
    \[ r(\mathrm{SNR}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_{\rm th}(\mathrm{SNR}) - \mu_{\rm sim}(\mathrm{SNR})} {\sigma_{\rm sim}(\mathrm{SNR})} \]
    • Result: most points lie within \(\pm 1\sigma\) for \(|\mathrm{SNR}|\lesssim 2\).
    • Gate: points with \(|r|>1\) are discarded (theory not accurate there) and are not used to train the GPR.
    • Beyond: larger deviations in the high-SNR tails (rare peaks/voids).

    ⟹ Validated domain for cosmo-SLICS: \(|\mathrm{SNR}|\le 2\); train GPR only on retained points.

    Gaussian progress regression validation

    Definition & criterion
    \[ \%\mathrm{err}_i(k)=100\times \frac{d^{\rm pred}_i(k)-d^{\rm real}_i(k)}{d^{\rm real}_i(k)}, \quad k:\ \mathrm{SNR\ bin} \]
    • Scan all cosmologies and all SNR bins.
    • Pass: small, residuals; most bins within combined uncertainties.
    • Validated range: typically \(|\mathrm{SNR}|\le 2\).
    • Outliers (\(|\%\mathrm{err}|> \) tolerance) are flagged for follow-up / exclusion.

    ⟹ This verifies the GPR captures the DV dependence over the grid before inference.

    LDT

    From Rare Events to the Full Distribution

    • The probability distribution function (PDF) \( P(x) \) describes how likely different values of a field are - e.g. densities in the Universe.
    • Its tails correspond to rare events (very high or low values).
    • Large-Deviation Theory focuses precisely on those tails - it predicts how fast the probability decays for rare fluctuations.

    \[ P(x) \; \sim\; e^{-\,\Psi(x)} \quad \text{with } \Psi(x) \text{ the rate function.} \]

    • Once \( \Psi(x) \) is known, the entire PDF shape can be reconstructed - including its most probable and extreme values.
    • ⟹ Knowing how the tails behave tells us the full statistical structure of the field.

    Large-Deviation Theory and its Validity

    • LDT provides an analytical framework to predict the one-point PDF of $\kappa$ from first principles.
    • Valid in the quasi-linear regime: intermediate scales where perturbation theory fails but non-linear collapse is moderate.
    • Breaks down at small scales where baryons and non-linear coupling dominate.
    • Assumes statistical isotropy and no baryonic feedback — both under future investigation.

    Physical Meaning of the Wavelet $\ell_1$-Norm

    • The $\ell_1$-norm measures the absolute amplitude of fluctuations at each scale → sensitive to non-Gaussian tails.
    • Acts as a proxy for the variance of the modulus distribution in each wavelet band.
    • Captures skewness and higher-order features encoded in the tail structure of the convergence PDF.
    • Physically linked to matter clustering and growth of structure — especially sensitive to $\Omega_m$ and $\sigma_8$.
