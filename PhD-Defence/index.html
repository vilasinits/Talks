Aim of this thesis

Advance cosmological inference beyond two-point statistics by developing theoretical and computational tools for weak lensing.

The Big Picture - From Theory to Inference

Build an end-to-end pipeline for weak lensing cosmology - going from theory to inference. This requires three key ingredients:

  • 1. Theory - analytic prediction of the wavelet $\ell_1$-norm

  • 2. Emulator - fast surrogate for weak-lensing $\kappa$ maps

  • 3. Validation - testing and calibration against simulations

Each part connects directly to this overarching goal and represents one stage of the pipeline.

Backup — Mass-Sheet Degeneracy

Statement
The lensing observables (shear shapes) are invariant under \[ \kappa(\theta) \;\longrightarrow\; \kappa'(\theta) = \lambda\,\kappa(\theta) + (1-\lambda) \] i.e. adding a constant “mass-sheet” while rescaling leaves the reduced shear unchanged.
Physical meaning
Lensing by a uniform sheet produces pure isotropic magnification, which is degenerate with a global rescaling of the convergence field. Without additional information (e.g. magnification, absolute size priors, redshift leverage), \(\kappa\) is only known up to this transformation.
Implication for inference
Cosmology from shear alone must rely on contrasts and statistics insensitive to an additive sheet, or break the degeneracy via external information.

From Rare Events to the Full Distribution

  • The probability distribution function (PDF) \( P(x) \) describes how likely different values of a field are - e.g. densities in the Universe.
  • Its tails correspond to rare events (very high or low values).
  • Large-Deviation Theory focuses precisely on those tails - it predicts how fast the probability decays for rare fluctuations.

\[ P(x) \; \sim\; e^{-\,\Psi(x)} \quad \text{with } \Psi(x) \text{ the rate function.} \]

  • Once \( \Psi(x) \) is known, the entire PDF shape can be reconstructed - including its most probable and extreme values.
  • ⟹ Knowing how the tails behave tells us the full statistical structure of the field.

Asymmetry of $\delta$ and its imprint on wavelet $\ell_{1}$

\[ \delta = \frac{\rho - \bar\rho}{\bar\rho}, \qquad \delta \ge -1 \quad(\rho\rightarrow 0), \qquad \delta \to +\infty \quad(\rho\gg\bar\rho) \]

  • Gravitational collapse produces unbounded high–density peaks, but voids cannot go below −1.
  • This asymmetry yields larger wavelet amplitudes in the positive tail than in the negative tail.
  • Thus the wavelet $\ell_1$-norm at a given scale is dominated by rare collapsed peaks, not by the underdense regions.

Interpreting wavelet $\ell_{1}$-derivatives ($\theta_1 \approx 22.5'$)

  • $\Omega_m$: increasing $\Omega_m$ pushes mass into collapsed halos → tails gain probability (positive lobes at large $|\kappa|$) and bulk loses probability (negative around $\kappa\!\approx\!0$).
  • $A_s$: increasing $A_s$ boosts moderate fluctuations more than rare peaks → center increases (positive near $\kappa{=}0$) and tails weaken (negative at large $|\kappa|$).
  • Ωₘ has the largest amplitude because this scale is dominated by non-linear collapsed structure, and Ωₘ directly controls how much mass ends up in halos - increasing Ωₘ strongly boosts the tail amplitude of κ.
  • Aₛ and Mν do not create new collapsed mass: Aₛ mainly rescales the linear amplitude and Mν suppresses growth by free-streaming, so both have a much weaker impact on the tail-driven ℓ₁ signal at this scale.

What is a “mildly non-linear” scale?

  • Defined by fluctuation amplitude: linear if \( \sigma(R)\!\ll\!1 \), mildly non-linear if \( \sigma(R)\!\sim\!0.5{-}1 \) (equivalently \( \Delta^2(k)\!\equiv\!k^3P(k)/(2\pi^2)\sim\mathcal{O}(1) \)).
  • For weak lensing with sources at \(z_s\!\approx\!2\), the efficiency kernel \(W(z)\propto (1+z)\,\chi(z)\big[1-\chi(z)/\chi_s\big]\) peaks at \(z_\text{peak}\approx 0.97\) (here: \( \chi(z_\text{peak})\approx 3.23\times10^3\) Mpc).
  • Angular scale \(\theta\) probes a comoving scale near the peak: \[ R \approx \chi\!\left(z_\text{peak}\right)\,\theta \quad(\theta\ \text{in radians}). \] Example: $\theta = 15'$ $\Rightarrow$ $R \approx 14.1\ \mathrm{Mpc}$ at the kernel peak ($z\simeq 0.97$).

Large-Deviation Theory and its Validity

  • LDT provides an analytical framework to predict the one-point PDF of $\kappa$ from first principles.
  • Valid in the quasi-linear regime: intermediate scales where perturbation theory fails but non-linear collapse is moderate.
  • Breaks down at small scales where baryons and non-linear coupling dominate.
  • Assumes statistical isotropy and no baryonic feedback - both under future investigation.

Filters

Why filter at all?

Core motivations
  • Pixel domain mixes scales → hard to attribute physics by scale.
  • Filtering = change of basis → separates spatial frequencies; reduces mode mixing.
  • Non-stationarity: WL κ is scale-variant & non-Gaussian → need localization in space+scale.
  • Sparsity: right basis concentrates energy in few coeffs → higher SNR for HOS tails.

Fourier vs Gaussian vs Starlet

Comparative view
  • Fourier: perfect spectral selectivity, but global (no spatial localization).
  • Gaussian: low-pass; simple, but mixes nearby scales and blurs features.
  • Starlet (à trous): isotropic, undecimated, shift-invariant multiscale bands.
  • Update rules: \( c_{j+1}=c_j * h_j \), \( w_{j+1}=c_j - c_{j+1} \); reconstruct with \( \sum_j w_j + c_J \).

Does filtering bias inference?

Inference integrity
  • Use theory-tracked summaries (e.g., wavelet \( \ell_1 \) per scale) with a modelled covariance.
  • Filtering is part of the summary definition; inference remains unbiased if the forward model matches.
  • Diagnostics: reconstruction error, cross-scale leakage, and null tests on Gaussian mocks.
Stress that any compression is safe if the forward model & covariance reflect the same pipeline.

Learning optimal filters vs fixed multiscale starlet

  • Learned filters in cosmology: – D’Isanto & Polsterer (2018), “Deep learning photometric redshifts” (A&A 620 A87). – Peter & McQuinn (2016), “Deep network cosmological parameter inference from convergence maps” (MNRAS 475 894).
  • Trade-offs of learned filters: – Address one task but become ©training-dataset dependent. – Lose scale-by-scale interpretability and analytic covariance modelling.
  • Why we retain the starlet: – Isotropic, undecimated multiresolution bands (clear physics per scale) – Fast algorithmic complexity O(N J) – Amenable to analytic summary–modelling and systematic control for pipelines like Euclid.

Why $\ell_1$ instead of $\ell_2$ on wavelet coefficients?

  • Robust to tails: $\ell_2$ squares values → dominated by rare extremes; $\ell_1$ grows linearly → preserves cosmological tail information.
  • Sparsity: non-Gaussian structure is sparse in wavelets; $\ell_1$ concentrates information, $\ell_2$ dilutes it across many small modes.
  • Noise behaviour: shape noise is small-amplitude; $\ell_2$ amplifies it quadratically, $\ell_1$ keeps it sub-dominant.
  • Cost vs alignment: $\ell_1$ is harder to optimise (non-smooth), but its geometry matches the physics of the field we measure.

→ $\ell_1$ maximizes cosmological signal-to-noise on non-Gaussian structure.

PDF vs wavelet $\ell_1$: differentiability matters

Key distinction
  • Histogram / PDF (measured) is a piecewise-constant functional of samples → zero/undefined gradients at bin edges.
  • Wavelet $\ell_1 = \sum |w_i|$ is sub-differentiable everywhere.
  • Thus, $\ell_1$ can flow gradients in any differentiable forward / inverse pipeline; PDFs cannot without reparametrization tricks.
  • Interpretation: PDF is a descriptive statistic; $\ell_1$ is also an optimizable functional of the field.
  • For theory-based inference, $\ell_1$ is easier to model, differentiate, and propagate through analytic pipelines.

What has been done for the PDF covariance — and what this implies for $\ell_{1}$

  • Build a semi-analytic model for the PDF covariance (halo-model / perturbative) and calibrate it against simulations (Sun+2022).
  • Use simulation ensembles to capture non-Gaussian off-diagonals and correct the theory in the tails where sampling noise is largest (D’Amico+2020).
  • Combine theory+simulation in a hybrid covariance to avoid the known failure of Gaussian covariance for 1-pt summaries.
  • Implication for our work: the same steps (theory core + simulation correction) can be applied to build a full covariance model for the wavelet $\ell_{1}$-norm.
